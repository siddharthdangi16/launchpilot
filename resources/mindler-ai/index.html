Mindler.AI: The Working Mom Who Left IBM to Build a Personal AI Assistant

Sandhya Pradhan had what most people would call a good problem to have: a senior lead role at IBM, a stable career, and a young family. She left it anyway to build Mindler.AI.

Leaving a comfortable, established corporate career is a hard decision for anyone. Doing it as a working mother adds a different layer of risk calculus entirely — the math isn't just "can I afford to lose this income," it's weighed against caregiving responsibilities, family expectations, and a society that often has a lot to say about a mother choosing to take on more uncertainty rather than less.

From corporate lead to founder

A senior role at IBM comes with structure, resources, and a clear path forward — the opposite of what early-stage founding actually offers. Sandhya's background gave her real operational and technical grounding, the kind that comes from years inside a large, complex organization, which she brought directly into building Mindler.AI rather than starting completely from scratch on instinct alone.

Building a personal AI assistant from a place of real need

Mindler.AI is built as a personal AI assistant — a category with no shortage of competition, but also a category where a founder's own lived experience of needing more support, more structure, and more help managing competing demands is a genuine advantage in product intuition. Building something to help people manage their own time and mental load lands differently when the founder herself is living the exact problem the product is meant to solve.

The "comfortable job" isn't always the safer choice. Sometimes the riskier-looking decision is the one that actually matches what you're capable of, once you stop optimizing for what looks safe to everyone else.

Reaching product-market fit, bootstrapped

Mindler.AI has reached product-market fit without outside funding — meaning real users found enough value in the product to keep using it, validated without the cushion of investor capital to paper over a weak signal. For a founder who left a stable income to build this, reaching that point bootstrapped is the clearest possible evidence that the decision to leave wasn't just brave — it was right.

Building inside a structured cohort

Sandhya's path through LaunchPilot's Founder MBA sits alongside founders at very different life stages — recent grads, career-switchers, deep-tech builders — giving a founder balancing motherhood and a company a structured cohort and founder-mentors to lean on, rather than navigating the transition entirely alone.
