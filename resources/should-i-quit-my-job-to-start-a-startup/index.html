Should I Quit My Job to Start a Startup?

LaunchPilot Resources 7 min read Updated June 2026

Most advice on this question is either "follow your passion" or a checklist of savings targets. Neither actually tells you whether now is the right moment. Here's a more useful way to think about it.

This is one of the most-searched questions among aspiring founders, and most of what comes back is a list of 9-12 things to "consider" — savings, runway, risk tolerance — without ever giving you a way to actually combine those factors into a decision. So instead of a checklist, here's a real framework: three signals to check, in order, before you hand in your resignation.

Signal 1: Do you have evidence, or just enthusiasm?

Quitting on the strength of an idea you're excited about, with zero outside validation, is the single most common mistake. Before money or timing even enters the conversation, ask whether you have actual evidence the idea works — not whether you believe in it.

You're not ready to quit yet if...
  • Your only validation is friends and family saying "that's a great idea"
  • You haven't talked to a single stranger in your target market
  • Nobody has paid you anything yet — not a pre-order, not a deposit, nothing
You have a real signal if...
  • You've had 15-20+ conversations with people outside your network who confirmed the problem is real
  • Someone has actually paid you — even a small amount, even a manual/concierge version of the product
  • You're already at capacity building this on the side, and the side-project hours have become the bottleneck

Signal 2: Can you survive the gap between "promising" and "profitable"?

This is the part most articles reduce to "save 6-12 months of expenses," which is reasonable but incomplete. The more useful question is whether you can survive the specific gap your business will have — which depends on your model, not a generic savings rule.

  • If you're building something with a long sales cycle (enterprise software, anything requiring procurement approval), assume your runway needs to be longer than you think — 12-18 months minimum.
  • If you're building something with a fast feedback loop (consumer product, D2C, anything people can buy in a day), 6-9 months of runway is often enough to know if it's working.
  • Either way, separate "business expenses" from "personal living expenses" in your math — most people who run out of runway early forgot to budget for both, not just one.

Signal 3: Do you actually need to quit, or do you need structure?

This is the signal almost nobody talks about. A lot of "should I quit" anxiety isn't really about money — it's about not having a clear next step, a deadline, or anyone to be accountable to once you're solo. Quitting doesn't solve that. Structure does.

If what's actually missing is momentum, mentorship, or a forcing function — not capital — the better first move might not be quitting at all. It might be joining a structured, part-time program that gives you deadlines, peer accountability, and access to people who've actually built something, while you're still employed and still have income. Quitting becomes the second decision, made later, once you have real traction — not the first decision, made on faith.

Quitting isn't a strategy. It's a consequence of already having a reason to.

Putting it together

If you have real validation (Signal 1), enough runway for your specific business model (Signal 2), and you've already tried structure and still hit a ceiling that only quitting can solve (Signal 3) — that's a genuinely informed decision to quit. If you're missing any of the three, the better next step usually isn't resignation. It's closing that specific gap first.
