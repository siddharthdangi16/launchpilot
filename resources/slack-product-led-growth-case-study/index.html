Slack's Pivot: From a Failed Game Studio to Product-Led Growth

LaunchPilot Resources 6 min read Updated June 2026
The MBA concept

Product-led growth (PLG) — a go-to-market strategy where the product itself, through ease of use and natural virality within teams, drives adoption and revenue, rather than a traditional outbound sales force.

Slack wasn't designed to be a messaging company. It was an internal tool, built so a struggling game studio's own employees could communicate better — while the actual game was failing.

Before Slack, Stewart Butterfield's team was building a multiplayer game called Glitch. The game didn't find a sustainable audience and the company faced winding down. But internally, the team had built their own communication tool to coordinate development — and that internal tool turned out to be the thing other people actually wanted, more than the game itself ever was.

The product became the go-to-market strategy

Rather than building a traditional enterprise sales motion — cold outreach, demos, long sales cycles — Slack's growth leaned heavily on the product spreading itself: one person on a team would start using it, invite teammates, and the value of the tool increased as more of a team joined, creating organic, bottom-up adoption inside companies before any official purchasing decision was made by management.

This is the essence of product-led growth: instead of a sales team convincing a decision-maker the product is valuable, the product proves its own value directly to the people who'll use it daily, and adoption spreads through usage rather than persuasion.

Why this only works if the product earns it

Product-led growth isn't a marketing trick you can apply to any product — it requires a product genuinely good enough that people want to bring it into their workplace voluntarily, and easy enough to start using without training or a formal rollout. Slack's emphasis on simplicity, fast onboarding, and immediate utility wasn't incidental; it was the entire mechanism the growth strategy depended on.

A product-led growth strategy is really a bet that your product is good enough to sell itself — which means the product has to come first, and the strategy second.

The pivot required admitting the original plan failed

The harder part of this story isn't the growth mechanic — it's the willingness to recognize that the thing the team had spent years building (the game) wasn't the business, and the thing they'd built almost as an afterthought (the internal tool) was. That kind of pivot requires genuinely separating ego and sunk cost from what the evidence is actually showing.

The lesson for a founder building today

Pay attention to what people actually do with what you've built, not just what you intended them to use. Sometimes the real business is sitting inside the thing you built to support your original idea, not the original idea itself — and noticing that requires being willing to let go of the version of the company you'd already mentally committed to.

Let the product do the convincing.
