Battery sustainability is one of the harder problems to build a startup around — it sits at the intersection of hardware, climate policy, and capital-intensive R&D. Devyansh Agarwal built Devananta to take it on anyway.

Devananta is one of the more technically ambitious companies to come out of LaunchPilot's cohorts — most Founder MBA companies are software or D2C, where the path from idea to first customer is measured in weeks. Battery sustainability is a different category entirely: longer R&D cycles, regulatory complexity, and a need for capital that doesn't come from a credit card or bootstrapped revenue. That made the company's path to funding — $200K in seed capital, secured through government grants rather than traditional venture rounds — a genuinely different playbook than most of his cohort-mates were running.

Why government grants, not VC

For climate and hardware-adjacent startups, government grant programs are often a more realistic first capital source than venture funding — they're built specifically to de-risk exactly the kind of long-horizon, high-capex research that traditional VCs are structurally reluctant to fund at the earliest stage. Securing $200K this way isn't a smaller achievement than a VC round; it's a different, and in some ways more difficult, fundraising motion — one that requires navigating government application processes, technical review committees, and policy alignment rather than a pitch deck and a term sheet.

Building inside a structured cohort

Devananta moved through LaunchPilot's Founder MBA alongside founders building in completely different categories — D2C brands, AI tools, marketplaces — which is itself a useful pressure-test for a deep-tech founder: explaining a technically complex sustainability problem clearly enough that a room of non-specialist founders and mentors can engage with it is a different skill than writing a grant application, and one that ends up mattering just as much when it's time to talk to investors, partners, or customers later.

Not every founder story is software shipped in a weekend. Some are multi-year bets on hard problems — and those deserve a structure too.

Where Devananta fits in LaunchPilot's broader portfolio

Devananta is a useful reminder that "startup MBA" doesn't only mean fast-moving consumer apps. The same cohort structure, mentor access, and founder-first teaching model that helped a plant-brand founder in Bengaluru also supports a battery sustainability founder in Mumbai — different timelines, different capital sources, same underlying support system.
