Suraj Reddy had the kind of job a lot of NYU graduates spend years trying to land in the US. He left it to build a home recovery and preventive care brand in Dubai instead.

The standard immigrant-founder narrative usually runs the other direction — someone leaves home to chase opportunity in the US. Suraj's path reversed that: a stable, comfortable position in the United States, walked away from voluntarily, to go build something of his own in a market most of his peers probably weren't even considering.

Why "cushy" is the right word, and why it matters

The hardest version of "should I quit my job to start a startup" isn't quitting a bad job — it's quitting a genuinely good one. A comfortable US role post-NYU likely came with real compensation, stability, and the kind of resume credibility a lot of people spend their twenties chasing. Walking away from that, specifically, is a different and harder decision than leaving something you were already unhappy in.

Cureeasy: home recovery and preventive care, built for Dubai

Cureeasy is positioned as Dubai's home recovery and preventive care solution — addressing the same underlying gap that drives healthcare-at-home demand everywhere: people recovering from procedures or managing ongoing health needs who would rather receive consistent, quality care at home than navigate repeated clinical visits. Building this specifically for Dubai means designing around a market with its own regulatory environment, expat-heavy demographics, and healthcare expectations distinct from either the US market Suraj left or the broader South Asian markets many LaunchPilot founders build for.

Choosing a market nobody handed you is a different kind of risk than choosing an idea nobody's tried. Suraj took on both at once.

Revenue generating, without outside capital

Cureeasy has reached real, generating revenue while remaining bootstrapped — meaningful in a category like home healthcare, where trust, logistics, and care quality all have to work simultaneously before a customer pays at all, let alone pays again. Doing that without investor capital to subsidize early losses is a strong signal that the underlying model works.

Building inside a structured cohort

Cureeasy moved through LaunchPilot's Founder MBA alongside founders building across very different markets and categories — useful context for a founder building specifically for the UAE, where learning from peers solving similar trust-and-logistics problems elsewhere translates directly, even when the underlying market looks nothing alike on the surface.
