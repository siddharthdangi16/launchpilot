Netflix's Business Model Pivot: From DVDs to Streaming to Disruptor

LaunchPilot Resources 7 min read Updated June 2026
The MBA concept

Self-disruption / business model pivot — the discipline of replacing your own most profitable business model before a competitor or new technology forces you to, even when the new model cannibalizes the old one.

Most companies get disrupted by someone else. Netflix is one of the rare examples of a company disrupting itself — twice — while it was still winning.

Netflix's first business model was DVD-by-mail, competing against Blockbuster's late-fee-driven retail model. It won that fight decisively. Then, while the DVD business was still healthy and profitable, Netflix made the decision to build streaming — a business model that would eventually cannibalize the very DVD subscriptions funding the company.

1997Founded as a DVD-by-mail rental service, competing directly against Blockbuster's late-fee model with a subscription, no-late-fee alternative.
2007Launches streaming as an add-on to existing DVD subscriptions — a smaller, less profitable business at the time, layered on top of the model that was actually making money.
2011+Streaming becomes the core product. Netflix begins investing heavily in original content, shifting from a distributor of other studios' content to a studio and platform in its own right.

Why cannibalize your own winning business

The instinct in a company with a profitable, growing core business is to protect it — to treat any internal proposal that threatens existing revenue as a risk to be managed rather than a strategy to pursue. Netflix's leadership made a different call: betting that if streaming was going to happen eventually, due to changing technology and consumer behavior, it was better for Netflix to be the one building it and eating its own DVD revenue, rather than waiting for someone else to do it to them.

This is the core idea behind disruption theory: incumbents rarely get beaten because they lack the resources or technology to respond. They get beaten because responding requires cannibalizing a profitable existing business, and most organizations are structurally unwilling to do that to themselves until it's too late.

From distributor to studio — a second, harder pivot

The shift into original content was arguably a bigger bet than the move to streaming itself. Owning a streaming pipe is one thing; becoming a studio that produces its own shows and films is an entirely different business, with different talent relationships, different cost structures, and a fundamentally different competitive set. Netflix made that transition while the licensing-based streaming model was still working — the same self-disruption instinct applied a second time.

The hardest pivot isn't responding to a competitor. It's having the discipline to compete with your own most profitable product before someone else builds the thing that will.

The lesson for a founder building today

Most early-stage founders don't have an existing profitable business to protect, which makes this lesson feel abstract — but the underlying instinct shows up early anyway: clinging to the first version of a product or business model that started working, past the point where evidence suggests it should evolve. The discipline Netflix modeled at scale is worth practicing early: regularly ask whether the thing currently working is the thing that should keep working, or whether you're just protecting it out of habit.

Strategic discipline, applied to your own idea
