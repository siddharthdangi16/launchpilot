Vishesh Arora didn't start Aesthetic Essentials from zero. He inherited a decade-old family business and faced a harder question than most founders ever have to answer: how do you modernize something that already works, without breaking what made it successful in the first place?

Family businesses carry a different kind of risk than a fresh startup. There's existing revenue to protect, existing relationships with suppliers and customers built over years, and often a different generation's instincts about how the business should be run. Walking into that as the next generation and proposing a D2C transformation is a fundamentally different challenge than building something new with no legacy to navigate.

From legacy manufacturing to a D2C brand

Aesthetic Essentials is built around copper utensils — a category with deep roots in Indian households, traditionally sold through wholesale and retail relationships rather than directly to consumers online. Rebuilding that as a D2C brand meant rethinking not just the sales channel, but the entire way the product was presented: branding, photography, a real online storefront, and a direct relationship with the end customer that a traditional wholesale-distribution model never required.

Why family business transformation is its own category of hard

Building a D2C brand from scratch means making every decision for the first time, with no existing stakeholders to convince. Transforming a family business means doing that same work while also managing the expectations of people who've run the business successfully for over a decade — proving that a new model can work without simply dismissing the model that came before it.

Some founders build something from nothing. Others have to convince something that already works to become something different — and that's its own kind of difficult.

₹10+ crore and 150+ SKUs later

The result speaks for itself: a business now doing over ₹10 crore a year in revenue, with 150+ products in its catalog — evidence that the D2C transformation didn't just modernize the brand's image, it meaningfully grew the business beyond what the original wholesale model had reached.

Building inside a structured cohort

Vishesh's path through LaunchPilot's Founder MBA sits alongside founders building from zero in software, deep-tech, and other categories — a useful contrast for a founder navigating the specific, less-discussed challenges of generational business transformation: succession dynamics, legacy operations, and modernizing a brand's identity without losing the trust it had already built.
