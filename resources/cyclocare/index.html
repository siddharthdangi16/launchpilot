Cyclo Care: A Period Tracking App Built for Rural India

Most period-tracking apps are built in a city, for a city — designed around a smartphone-fluent, urban user with reliable data and prior exposure to similar apps. Manish Charhate built Cyclo Care for a completely different user, and that decision is the whole story.

Manish, based in Bengaluru, built Cyclo Care specifically for women in rural and Tier 3 India — a market most consumer health apps don't design for at all, let alone design for first. That's not a small distinction. A product built for a metro user and casually "expanded" to rural markets later tends to fail there, because the actual constraints — data costs, language, prior digital literacy, cultural openness to discussing the topic at all — are different enough that they have to be designed around from day one, not patched in afterward.

Designing for a market most apps ignore

Period tracking as a category already carries cultural sensitivity in many parts of India — in smaller towns and rural areas specifically, the topic can carry more stigma, less open family conversation, and less prior exposure to health-tracking apps generally, compared to urban counterparts. Building a product that women in this market would actually trust enough to download, use, and keep using required treating those constraints as the design brief, not as a footnote.

Why "built for rural India" is a genuine technical founder's problem

A founder with a technical background — Manish came in as a Bengaluru techie — brings a useful lens to this kind of product: building lightweight, low-data, reliable software that works on the kind of devices and connectivity actually common in smaller towns, rather than assuming the high-end-smartphone, always-online conditions common in a city like Bengaluru itself. That gap between where a founder lives and who they're building for is exactly the kind of distance that's easy to underestimate.

The hardest part of building for an underserved market usually isn't the technology. It's resisting the instinct to build for the user you understand best, instead of the user who actually needs it most.

1,00,000+ downloads, no outside funding

Reaching six figures in downloads without venture funding is a meaningful signal on its own — it means real word-of-mouth, real retained usage, and a product that resonated enough with its intended audience to spread without a paid acquisition budget behind it. For a women's health product built for a market that's traditionally underserved by mainstream app design, that kind of organic reach is a strong proof point that the product genuinely fit the people it was built for.

Building inside a structured cohort

Cyclo Care moved through LaunchPilot's Founder MBA alongside founders building in very different categories — D2C brands, deep-tech, fintech — giving Manish structured exposure to broader business and growth thinking alongside the product instincts he already had as a technical founder.
